Research

The Effect of Income And Employment Premiums of Education on the Decision to Enrol in University

Many studies of individual decision to attend post-secondary education look at sociological variabilities. My research hypothesizes that one’s decision to attend post-secondary education can be explained using traditional financial models of risk and return. Using unemployment rates for risk and average earnings for return, I found a simply financial model can act a strong predictor for university and college attendance.

Aging, Risk Aversion, and Stock Investment

How does investing behavior change throughout one’s life? The conventional wisdom is to shift one’s allocation from stocks to bonds as they get older. However, my research shows this is not always the case. Using a novel econometric approach through non-parametric statistics I overcame many methodological challenges to demonstrate investment behavior across individual’s lives.

Can Higher Education Exams Be Shortened?

Do university exams need to be so long? Can the number of questions be reduced without sacrificing quality? Few have researched these questions in the past due to the complexity and difficulty of traditional methods. To answer these questions empirically, we developed a new approached. Our research shows strong evidence that in some courses, shortening of final exams to two hours satisfies the same indicators of exam quality as a three hour one.

Alberta Meat Demand Modelling

A complex consumer demand model was constructed to exam the demand for over 250 cuts of meat in Alberta. Price and income elasticities were estimated to help retailer’s better set optimal prices for their product. In addition, other demand factors were analyzed. Many products have a seasonal response that can be captured using average temperature. For example, kink in the demand curved was found at eleven degrees Celsius for steak.
